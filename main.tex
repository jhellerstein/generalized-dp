\begin{abstract}
  Dynamic programming succeeds when every subproblem admits a compact
  summary that is sufficient for all future compositions.
  Identifying the right summary is a domain-by-domain art:
  Selinger's ``interesting orders'' in query optimization and
  property-aware extraction in equality saturation are two prominent
  examples.
  We propose an abstract framework---\emph{semantic dynamic
  programming}---in which subproblems carry a shrinking set of
  semantically relevant properties, and memoization is indexed by
  bundles of those properties; when properties are ordered by
  refinement, the bundle domain is a canonical join-semilattice
  completion.
  We give sufficient conditions under which the lifted recurrence
  satisfies Bellman's Principle of Optimality exactly, show that the
  bundle domain is the free join-semilattice over the refinement
  poset, and recover Selinger's algorithm as the discrete instance.
  We then state the results we aim to prove: a universality theorem
  characterizing Bellman's principle by the existence of a semantic
  factorization, and a minimality theorem identifying the
  continuation-equivalence quotient as the canonical such
  factorization.
  On this account every compositionally presented discrete
  optimization problem can be lifted to satisfy Bellman's principle;
  what varies is the price.
  We accordingly propose \emph{width} and \emph{dimension}---the
  size of a minimal sufficient Pareto frontier and the number of
  properties needed to index it---as intrinsic complexity measures
  of discrete optimization problems.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Bellman's Principle of Optimality is usually stated operationally: an
optimal solution is composed of optimal solutions to subproblems.
The principle holds or fails depending on how subproblems are
identified.
When it fails, skilled practitioners have repeatedly rediscovered
the same remedy---\emph{refine the subproblem state} until the
principle holds again---each time as an isolated trick for the
problem at hand.
The remedy has no name, no general formulation, and no theory
telling us when it applies or what refinement suffices.

The canonical example is Selinger's dynamic program for join
ordering~\cite{selinger1979access}.
Memoizing only the cheapest plan per subexpression violates the
principle: a slightly more expensive plan whose output is sorted may
enable a much cheaper merge join later.
Selinger's remedy was to index the memo table by
\emph{interesting orders}: output properties that some future
operator can exploit.
Equality saturation exhibits the same phenomenon.
Extraction from an e-graph~\cite{willsey2021egg} that memoizes one
cheapest term per e-class is exact only when cost is compositional on
e-classes; richer objectives (streamability, partitioning,
provenance) require the extractor to retain a frontier of
representatives per class, indexed by the properties that enclosing
contexts can observe.
The nearest thing to a named version of the remedy appears in
reinforcement learning and control, where \emph{state
augmentation}~\cite{sutton2018reinforcement} enlarges the state
until the Markov property holds.
That practice confirms the pattern but supplies no theory:
the augmentation is devised by insight, problem by problem, with no
characterization of when a proposed augmentation is sufficient,
minimal, or canonical.

These fixes are invariably presented as domain-specific engineering.
The aim of this paper is to exhibit the mathematics they share---and,
with it, the missing characterization.
Informally, we claim:

\begin{quote}
  \emph{Every} discrete optimization problem---once its solutions
  are presented compositionally, as assemblies of solutions to
  subproblems---can be made to satisfy Bellman's principle.
  The trick is twofold: expose sufficiently expressive
  \emph{semantic properties} of partial solutions, and defer
  commitment by retaining a sufficiently large frontier of
  Pareto-optimal (non-dominated) partial solutions per subgoal.
\end{quote}

The mechanism beneath the trick is a factorization: a dynamic
program satisfies Bellman's principle exactly when its subproblem
summaries factor through a domain of \emph{semantic property
bundles}, where each subproblem carries a monotonically shrinking
set of properties that remain relevant to its future.
Because sufficiency can always be achieved---in the worst case the
properties describe partial solutions completely and the frontier
retains nearly everything---the substantive question is
quantitative: \emph{how large must the frontier be?}
We take the size and dimensionality of a minimal sufficient frontier
to be intrinsic complexity measures of a discrete optimization
problem; Section~\ref{sec:width} makes them precise and poses their
characterization as a central goal.

This section develops each ingredient of that claim carefully, with
Selinger's algorithm as a running example and e-graph extraction as
a parallel thread; Table~\ref{tab:instances} at the end of the
section summarizes how both instantiate the framework.
We first work in a \emph{Boolean} model, where properties are
independent of one another; Section~\ref{sec:refinement} then
refines the model to allow properties that subsume one another.
Section~\ref{sec:results} states the theorems we intend to prove.
The mathematics required is modest: finite sets, partial orders, and
induction carry most of the development, and the two lightweight
algebraic notions we lean on---join-semilattices and quotients by
equivalence relations---are defined where they first appear.

\subsection{Decomposition Systems}
\label{sec:decomposition}

We begin with the combinatorial skeleton of a dynamic program: what
the subproblems are and how solutions compose.
No costs and no properties yet.
A decomposition system names the subproblems (\emph{subgoals}), the
ways a solution to a subproblem is assembled from solutions to
smaller subproblems (\emph{constructors}), and the subproblem we
ultimately care about (the \emph{goal}).

\begin{definition}[Decomposition system]
\label{def:decomposition}
A \emph{decomposition system} is a triple
$(X,\mathcal R,\goal)$ where
\begin{enumerate}
\item $X$ is a finite set of \emph{subgoals};
\item $\mathcal R$ is a finite set of \emph{constructors}, each of
  the form
  \[
  r : (x_1,\ldots,x_{k_r}) \to x
  \]
  with $x, x_1,\ldots,x_{k_r}\in X$ and arity $k_r\ge 1$; and
\item $\goal\in X$ is the \emph{goal}.
\end{enumerate}
We require acyclicity: the directed graph on $X$ with an edge
$x_i\to x$ for every constructor $r:(x_1,\ldots,x_{k_r})\to x$ and
every $i$ must have no directed cycle.
\end{definition}

Constructors induce a reachability order among subgoals: $x$ lies
below $y$ when $x$ can be used, perhaps through intermediate
constructions, in building $y$.

\begin{definition}[Subgoal order]
\label{def:subgoal-order}
Write $x \le_X y$ when there is a directed path from $x$ to $y$ in
the graph of Definition~\ref{def:decomposition} (including the empty
path, so $\le_X$ is reflexive).
By acyclicity, $(X,\le_X)$ is a finite poset.
\end{definition}

Solutions are terms: a solution to $x$ is either given outright
(\emph{primitive}) or assembled by a constructor from solutions to
its children.

\begin{definition}[Solutions]
\label{def:solutions}
Each subgoal $x\in X$ is equipped with a finite (possibly empty) set
$\Prim(x)$ of \emph{primitive solutions}.
The families $\Sol(x)$ of \emph{solutions} are defined inductively as
the least sets satisfying:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\Prim(x)\subseteq\Sol(x)$; and
\item if $r:(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x$ is a constructor and
  $s_i\in\Sol(x_i)$ for each $i$, then the formal term
  $r(s_1,\ldots,s_k)\in\Sol(x)$.
\end{enumerate}
By finiteness and acyclicity, every $\Sol(x)$ is finite.
We assume $\Sol(x)\neq\varnothing$ for every $x$ that participates in
some solution of the goal, and in particular
$\Sol(\goal)\neq\varnothing$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running example: a three-relation join]
\label{ex:query}
Consider the query
\[
\texttt{SELECT * FROM R, S, T WHERE R.a = S.a AND S.b = T.b}
\]
over relations $R,S,T$.
The subgoals are the connected subexpressions
\[
X = \{R,\; S,\; T,\; RS,\; ST,\; RST\},
\]
with goal $\goal = RST$.
(We omit the cross product $RT$ for brevity; nothing changes if it is
included.)
The constructors are, for each join subgoal, a nested-loop join and a
merge join in each input order; for instance
\[
\NL : (R,S)\to RS,
\qquad
\MJ : (R,S)\to RS,
\]
and similarly for $(S,T)\to ST$, $(RS,T)\to RST$, and
$(R,ST)\to RST$.
The primitive solutions are the access methods:
\[
\Prim(R)=\{R_\bot,\;R_a\},
\quad
\Prim(S)=\{S_\bot,\;S_a,\;S_b\},
\quad
\Prim(T)=\{T_\bot,\;T_b\},
\]
where $R_\bot$ is an unordered scan of $R$, $R_a$ is a scan of $R$
sorted on column $a$, and so on: seven access methods in all.
Join subgoals have no primitive solutions.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[E-graphs as decomposition systems]
\label{ex:egraph}
An e-graph is, in this vocabulary, a decomposition system.
Subgoals are e-classes.
Each e-node with child classes $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ belonging to class
$x$ is a constructor $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x$; e-nodes with no
children are primitive solutions.
The goal is the root e-class, and $\Sol(x)$ is exactly the set of
terms extractable from class $x$.
(E-graphs may contain cycles; here we restrict attention to acyclic
e-graphs, as is common in extraction.)
Ordinary extraction memoizes one cheapest term per class; the
framework developed below memoizes a \emph{frontier} of terms per
class, indexed by extraction-relevant properties.
This has a natural data-structure realization, the
\emph{mult-egraph} (defined in Section~\ref{sec:multegraph}): each
e-class is subdivided into subclasses of terms with equal semantic
labels, so that the memo-table distinctions of this paper live in
the graph itself rather than in a side table.
\end{example}

\subsection{Semantic Properties}
\label{sec:properties}

Solutions to the same subgoal may differ in ways that matter to the
contexts that consume them.
We model those differences with a finite set of properties; a
solution will later be labeled by the subset of properties it
provides.
For now properties are independent of one another; we enrich them
with a refinement order in Section~\ref{sec:refinement}.

\begin{definition}[Property domain]
\label{def:property-domain}
A \emph{property domain} is a finite set $O$, whose elements are
called \emph{semantic properties}.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Sort orders as properties]
\label{ex:discrete-properties}
In Example~\ref{ex:query} the properties are output sort orders on
the two join keys.
Because $R.a = S.a$ and $S.b = T.b$, we canonicalize each
equivalence class of columns to a single property, yielding the
property domain
\[
O = \{a,\; b\},
\]
where $a$ means ``output sorted on the $a$-key equivalence class''
and $b$ likewise.
Note that ``unordered'' is not a property; it is the absence of
properties, represented below by the empty bundle.
\end{example}

\subsection{Remaining Interest}
\label{sec:interest}

The property domain is global: it enumerates the properties that
exist.
Which properties \emph{matter} is local to the problem instance and
to the subgoal.
A property matters at subgoal $x$ only if some constructor applied
above $x$ can still exploit it.
As construction proceeds toward the goal, the constructors yet to be
applied form a shrinking set, so the properties they can exploit
shrink as well.
We package this as an assignment $I$ giving each subgoal $x$ the set
$I(x)$ of properties still relevant above $x$---the
\emph{undischarged obligations} of $x$.
Its two axioms formalize the intuitions just given.
Antitonicity is the formal content of the slogan ``interesting
orders shrink'': as subgoals grow toward the goal, the surrounding
context contracts, so fewer properties remain observable.
Exhaustion at the goal says that a completed solution has no future;
only its cost matters.

\begin{definition}[Remaining-interest assignment]
\label{def:interest}
A \emph{remaining-interest assignment} for a decomposition system
$(X,\mathcal R,\goal)$ over a property domain $O$ is a map
\[
I : X \to \mathcal P(O)
\]
that is \emph{antitone} (order-reversing) with respect to the
subgoal order:
\[
x\le_X y
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
I(y)\subseteq I(x),
\]
and \emph{exhausted at the goal}: $I(\goal)=\varnothing$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Interesting orders]
\label{ex:interest}
In Example~\ref{ex:query}, a sort order is relevant at a subgoal
exactly when a join predicate involving its key remains to be
applied above that subgoal:
\[
I(R)=\{a\},\quad
I(S)=\{a,b\},\quad
I(T)=\{b\},
\]
\[
I(RS)=\{b\},\quad
I(ST)=\{a\},\quad
I(RST)=\varnothing.
\]
For instance, after $RS$ is formed, the only remaining join is on the
$b$-key, so sortedness on $a$ can no longer be exploited:
$a\notin I(RS)$.
Antitonicity is visible along every edge, e.g.,
$I(RS)\subseteq I(R)\cup\{b\}$\,---\,more precisely
$I(RS)=\{b\}\subseteq I(S)$ and, along the $R$ branch,
$I(RS)\cap I(R)=\varnothing$ records that $R$'s obligation $a$ was
discharged by the join.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Determination reading]
\label{rem:determination}
Readers familiar with determination
theory~\cite{hellerstein2026determination} will recognize the shape
of Definition~\ref{def:interest}.
The set $I(x)$ plays the role of \emph{remaining ambiguity}:
the distinctions among solutions of $x$ that have not yet been
rendered irrelevant.
Extension of a subgoal acts like a commitment, irrevocably shrinking
that ambiguity; an ``interesting order'' is an undischarged
obligation, and choosing to retain representatives for each
obligation is a deferred commitment.
We keep the present development self-contained and return to this
correspondence in Section~\ref{sec:determination-bridge}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Property Bundles}
\label{sec:bundles}

The memo table at $x$ must answer for whatever the future may still
demand of $x$, and the future's demands come in combinations:
different continuations exploit different subsets of the remaining
obligations $I(x)$.
The memoization index is therefore not a single property but a
\emph{bundle} of properties: a set of obligations to be discharged
jointly.
Nothing requires a bundle to be satisfiable---in the running
example the bundle $\{a,b\}$ demands output sorted both ways, and
the memo table will simply record that no plan provides it.
(On the supply side, a single solution may also discharge several
obligations at once, as when an operator's output is both sorted on
one key and partitioned on another.)

\begin{definition}[Bundle domain]
\label{def:bundles}
For each subgoal $x$, the \emph{bundle domain} is
\[
Q_x = \mathcal P(I(x)),
\]
the subsets of the remaining-relevant properties, ordered by
inclusion, which we write $\sqsubseteq$.
The bottom element is $\varnothing$ (no properties provided) and the
binary join is union:
\[
q_1\sqcup q_2 = q_1\cup q_2 .
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Bundle domains]
\label{ex:bundles}
In the running example,
\[
Q_S=\bigl\{\varnothing,\;\{a\},\;\{b\},\;\{a,b\}\bigr\},
\qquad
Q_{RS}=\bigl\{\varnothing,\;\{b\}\bigr\},
\qquad
Q_{RST}=\{\varnothing\}.
\]
\end{example}

To motivate the next piece of machinery, imagine running the
construction bottom-up, as Selinger's optimizer does.
When a constructor assembles a plan for $RS$ from child plans for
$R$ and $S$, the physical properties of its output are computable
from those of its inputs: a merge join of $R_a$ and $S_a$ produces
output sorted on $a$.
But $a$ has just been discharged---no constructor above $RS$ can
exploit it---so carrying it in the label would burden the memo key
with a distinction no future can see.
Two plans for $RS$ that differ only in such dead properties should
occupy a single memo entry and compete on cost alone.
The operational discipline is therefore: compute the raw output
properties, then \emph{trim} them to the remaining interest of the
subgoal under construction.
The restriction maps are this trimming step; because interest only
shrinks toward the goal, trimming is always well defined.

\begin{definition}[Restriction maps]
\label{def:restriction}
For $x\le_X y$, define
\[
\rho_{x,y} : Q_x \to Q_y,
\qquad
\rho_{x,y}(D) = D\cap I(y).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Coherence of restriction]
\label{lem:restriction}
Each $\rho_{x,y}$ preserves joins and bottom, and the family is
functorial, i.e., restrictions compose as expected:
$\rho_{x,x}=\mathrm{id}$ and
$\rho_{y,z}\circ\rho_{x,y}=\rho_{x,z}$ whenever
$x\le_X y\le_X z$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from the definitions: intersection with a fixed set
$I(y)$ preserves unions and the empty set, and
$I(z)\subseteq I(y)$ gives functoriality.
\end{proof}

The bundle domains thus form a coherent family of
\emph{join-semilattices}---posets in which every pair of elements
has a least upper bound, or \emph{join}; here bundles are ordered by
inclusion and the join of two bundles is their union---connected by
the restriction maps.
This is the precise form of monotone shrinkage.

\subsection{Costs, Labels, and Frontiers}
\label{sec:costs}

We now attach quantitative and semantic data to solutions: each
solution gets a cost, and a \emph{label} recording the bundle of
remaining-relevant properties it provides.
Bundles thus appear in two roles: as \emph{demands} (requirement
bundles indexing the memo table) and as \emph{supplies} (labels of
solutions); a solution \emph{satisfies} a requirement bundle when
its supply covers the demand.
In Selinger's setting the label is the sort order a plan advertises;
in an e-graph it is the set of properties an analysis attaches to a
candidate term.

\begin{definition}[Costed, labeled solutions]
\label{def:labels}
A \emph{semantic labeling} of a decomposition system consists of, for
each subgoal $x$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a \emph{cost function}
  $c_x : \Sol(x)\to\Rbar$, where
  $\Rbar=[0,\infty]$; and
\item a \emph{label map}
  $\lambda_x : \Sol(x)\to Q_x$,
  assigning each solution the bundle of remaining-relevant properties
  it provides.
\end{enumerate}
A solution $s\in\Sol(x)$ \emph{satisfies} a bundle $q\in Q_x$ when
$q\sqsubseteq\lambda_x(s)$.
\end{definition}

The object a dynamic program computes is what Bellman's tradition
calls the \emph{value function}: here, the cost of the cheapest
solution providing at least a given bundle.
The only novelty relative to the classical notion is the index: the
value function takes a requirement bundle as well as a subgoal.

\begin{definition}[Value function]
\label{def:value}
For each subgoal $x$ and bundle $q\in Q_x$, define
\[
V_x(q)
=
\min\bigl\{\,c_x(s) : s\in\Sol(x),\; q\sqsubseteq\lambda_x(s)\,\bigr\},
\]
with $\min\varnothing = \infty$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Dominance and frontiers]
\label{def:dominance}
On costed bundles in $\Rbar\times Q_x$, say $(c,q)$ \emph{dominates}
$(c',q')$ when $c\le c'$ and $q'\sqsubseteq q$: cheaper, and at least
as capable.
The \emph{memo frontier} at $x$ is the set of dominance-maximal pairs
$\bigl(c_x(s),\lambda_x(s)\bigr)$ over $s\in\Sol(x)$, together with
witnessing solutions.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Cost--property Pareto tradeoff]
\label{rem:pareto}
A stronger label alone never licenses discarding a solution.
A weaker-labeled but cheaper solution is dominance-incomparable with
a stronger-labeled but costlier one, and both may be needed by
different futures.
The canonical memo object is therefore the frontier in
$\Rbar\times Q_x$, \emph{not} a single best solution per property.
Equivalently: the value function $V_x$ is antitone in $q$
($q\sqsubseteq q' \Rightarrow V_x(q)\le V_x(q')$), and the frontier
is a minimal representation of $V_x$.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Memo entries for $S$]
\label{ex:memo}
In the running example, $I(S)=\{a,b\}$ and the primitive solutions
give labels
\[
\lambda_S(S_\bot)=\varnothing,
\qquad
\lambda_S(S_a)=\{a\},
\qquad
\lambda_S(S_b)=\{b\}.
\]
So the memo table for $S$ has logical entries
\[
V_S(\varnothing)=c(S_\bot)\wedge c(S_a)\wedge c(S_b),
\quad
V_S(\{a\})=c(S_a),
\quad
V_S(\{b\})=c(S_b),
\quad
V_S(\{a,b\})=\infty,
\]
the last because a single output stream is sorted on both keys only
when the two orders happen to coincide on the data; no access method
here provides that.
This is precisely the Selinger phenomenon: the optimizer retains best
plans $S_\bot$, $S_a$, and $S_b$ simultaneously, because they are
dominance-incomparable.
\end{example}

\subsection{Semantic Constructors: the Bellman Condition}
\label{sec:constructors}

Everything so far describes a single subgoal in isolation.
Bellman's principle is a statement about \emph{composition}: it holds
when the behavior of a constructor depends on its children only
through their memoized summaries.
In our terms: two child solutions with the same label must be
interchangeable in every context, except that the cheaper one is
always at least as good.
The following definition---the heart of the framework---captures
this by requiring that both the label and the cost of a constructed
solution be computable from the labels and costs of its children,
via a \emph{property-transfer map} $F_r$ and a
\emph{cost-composition map} $G_r$.
This is the exact content of the Principle of Optimality, isolated
as a property of the labeling rather than of the algorithm.

\begin{definition}[Semantic factorization]
\label{def:factorization}
A semantic labeling \emph{factors} a decomposition system when for
every constructor $r:(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x$ there exist:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a monotone \emph{property-transfer map}
  \[
  F_r : Q_{x_1}\times\cdots\times Q_{x_k}\;\to\;Q_x
  \]
  such that for all $s_i\in\Sol(x_i)$,
  \[
  \lambda_x\bigl(r(s_1,\ldots,s_k)\bigr)
  =
  F_r\bigl(\lambda_{x_1}(s_1),\ldots,\lambda_{x_k}(s_k)\bigr);
  \]
\item a \emph{cost-composition map}
  \[
  G_r : (Q_{x_1}\times\Rbar)\times\cdots\times(Q_{x_k}\times\Rbar)
  \;\to\;\Rbar,
  \]
  monotone in each cost coordinate and antitone in each label
  coordinate, such that for all $s_i\in\Sol(x_i)$,
  \[
  c_x\bigl(r(s_1,\ldots,s_k)\bigr)
  =
  G_r\bigl(
  (\lambda_{x_1}(s_1),c_{x_1}(s_1)),
  \ldots,
  (\lambda_{x_k}(s_k),c_{x_k}(s_k))
  \bigr).
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[The term ``factorization'']
\label{rem:factors}
The word carries its standard mathematical meaning: a map $g$
\emph{factors through} a map $h$ when $g=f\circ h$ for some $f$,
i.e., $g$ can be computed from $h$'s output alone.
Condition (1) says that the composite-label map factors through the
child labelings, with $F_r$ as the factor:
\[
\lambda_x\circ r
=
F_r\circ(\lambda_{x_1}\times\cdots\times\lambda_{x_k}),
\]
and condition (2) says the same for cost, through labels and costs
jointly.
Equivalently: the relation ``has the same label'' is a
\emph{congruence} for every constructor---equivalent children yield
equal-labeled parents---and $F_r$, $G_r$ are the operations induced
on label classes.
This is the algebraic content of Bellman's principle: a summary on
which the operations of the algebra remain well defined.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why monotone?]
\label{rem:monotone}
Both variance conditions say the same thing: \emph{over-delivery of
properties is never punished}.
The memo answers a demand $q$ by covering---it returns the cheapest
solution providing \emph{at least} $q$---so the recurrence may feed
a constructor children that provide more than was demanded.
Monotonicity of $F_r$ ensures richer inputs never shrink the output
bundle; antitonicity of $G_r$ in the label coordinates ensures
richer inputs never raise the cost.
Both hold for classical fixed-algorithm operators, whose property
propagation and cost are positive in their inputs' properties: an
output property holds because input properties are preserved or
created, never because one is absent.
An \emph{adaptive} operator that switches algorithms based on input
properties can violate both; the remedy is to model each algorithm
as its own constructor, moving the choice into the search.
If the conditions fail anyway, memoization by exact label (the
general mult-egraph structure of Section~\ref{sec:multegraph})
remains available; the variance conditions are precisely the license
for the cheaper covering-based tables.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Transfer maps for joins]
\label{ex:transfer}
In the running example, with the bundle domains of
Example~\ref{ex:bundles}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Nested-loop join $\NL:(R,S)\to RS$ requires nothing of its
  inputs and (in the simplest model) destroys order:
  \[
  F_{\NL}(q_R, q_S) = \varnothing .
  \]
  (A variant that preserves the outer input's order is modeled by
  $F(q_R,q_S)=\rho(q_R)$, with $\rho$ the restriction into $Q_{RS}$.)
\item Merge join $\MJ:(R,S)\to RS$ is \emph{partial}: it is
  applicable only to child bundles providing the join key, i.e.,
  $a\in q_R$ and $a\in q_S$.
  Its output is sorted on $a$, but $a\notin I(RS)$, so after
  restriction
  \[
  F_{\MJ}(q_R,q_S) = \varnothing
  \quad(\text{or } \{b\} \text{ if the physical operator preserves a
  } b\text{-order of } S).
  \]
  Partial constructors are accommodated by splitting $r$ into one
  total constructor per admissible combination of child bundles, or
  equivalently by letting $G_r=\infty$ off the admissible region.
\end{itemize}
Cost composition $G_r$ is the usual estimated operator cost plus
child costs, which is monotone in child costs.
\end{example}

\subsection{Semantic Memoization and the Lifted Recurrence}
\label{sec:recurrence}

A factored labeling supports dynamic programming over bundles: to
meet a requirement $q$ at subgoal $x$, either a primitive solution
satisfies $q$ directly, or some constructor into $x$ can be fed
child bundles whose transferred output covers $q$, in which case the
children are obtained recursively at their cheapest.
The recurrence minimizes over both cases.

\begin{definition}[Lifted Bellman recurrence]
\label{def:recurrence}
Given a factored semantic labeling, define
$\widehat V_x : Q_x\to\Rbar$ for each subgoal $x$ by structural
recursion over the (acyclic) decomposition system:
\[
\widehat V_x(q)
=
\min\left(
\begin{aligned}
&\min\bigl\{\,c_x(s) : s\in\Prim(x),\ q\sqsubseteq\lambda_x(s)\,\bigr\},
\\[2pt]
&\min_{\substack{
r:(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x\\
q_i\in Q_{x_i}\;:\;q\sqsubseteq F_r(q_1,\ldots,q_k)
}}
G_r\bigl(
(q_1,\widehat V_{x_1}(q_1)),
\ldots,
(q_k,\widehat V_{x_k}(q_k))
\bigr)
\end{aligned}
\right).
\]
\end{definition}

In practice one enumerates only the child bundles realized on the
memo frontier; dominance pruning (Remark~\ref{rem:pareto}) keeps the
tables small.
At the goal, $Q_{\goal}=\{\varnothing\}$ and
$\widehat V_{\goal}(\varnothing)$ is the value of the optimization
problem; the corresponding witness is the extracted solution.
In e-graph terms, the recurrence is extraction with a per-class
frontier: instead of tabulating one cheapest term per e-class, the
extractor tabulates the cheapest term per property bundle.

\begin{example}[One turn of the crank]
\label{ex:crank}
We compute the memo entries for $RS$ in the running example.
Take toy scan costs
\[
c(R_\bot)=8,\quad c(R_a)=9,\qquad
c(S_\bot)=10,\quad c(S_a)=12,\quad c(S_b)=13,
\]
so the child tables read
$\widehat V_R(\varnothing)=8$, $\widehat V_R(\{a\})=9$,
$\widehat V_S(\varnothing)=10$, $\widehat V_S(\{a\})=12$,
$\widehat V_S(\{b\})=13$.
Suppose nested-loop join adds $20$ to the sum of its input costs and
merge join adds $5$, with the transfer maps of
Example~\ref{ex:transfer} (both joins produce
$\varnothing\in Q_{RS}$; merge join requires $a$ in both inputs).

For the requirement $q=\varnothing$:
\begin{itemize}
\item via $\NL$, any child bundles suffice, and taking the cheapest
  children gives $8+10+20=38$;
\item via $\MJ$, the only admissible child bundles are
  $q_R=\{a\}$, $q_S=\{a\}$, giving $9+12+5=26$.
\end{itemize}
So $\widehat V_{RS}(\varnothing)=26$, witnessed by the merge join of
sorted scans.
For the requirement $q=\{b\}$: no constructor into $RS$ produces a
bundle containing $b$, so $\widehat V_{RS}(\{b\})=\infty$.
The machinery has discovered that a $b$-ordered $RS$ is unrealizable
with this operator inventory: a merge join on $b$ at the top would
require enriching the constructors, say with an order-preserving
join variant or an explicit sort operator.
This is exactly the bookkeeping a Selinger optimizer performs; the
recurrence merely does it uniformly.
\end{example}

Theorem~\ref{thm:exactness} below asserts that
$\widehat V_x = V_x$: the lifted recurrence is exact.

\subsection{A Refinement of the Model: Ordered Properties}
\label{sec:refinement}

The Boolean model treats properties as independent.
In practice, properties often subsume one another: a table sorted
lexicographically on $(a,b)$ is in particular sorted on $a$, so a
solution providing the former satisfies every requirement on the
latter.
The Boolean model can encode subsumption only by convention---every
label and transfer map must remember to list all implied
properties---and nothing enforces that the convention is followed.
We now refine the model to make subsumption first-class: the
property domain acquires a partial order, and bundles are required
to be closed under it.
Everything established so far will carry over unchanged.

\begin{definition}[Ordered property domain]
\label{def:ordered-domain}
An \emph{ordered property domain} is a finite poset $(O,\preceq)$.
We read $a\preceq b$ as ``$b$ refines (subsumes) $a$'': any use of a
solution that is satisfied by property $a$ is also satisfied by
property $b$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Refinable properties]
\label{ex:refinable-properties}
Lexicographic sort orders give a nontrivial refinement order:
\[
\mathrm{sorted}(a)\;\preceq\;\mathrm{sorted}(a,b).
\]
Other naturally ordered property domains include partitioning
schemes (refined by co-partitioning), streamability, and
provenance-preservation.
\end{example}

Bundles must now respect refinement: a solution providing a property
implicitly provides everything that property subsumes, so a bundle
is a set closed downward under $\preceq$.
Such sets are determined by their maximal elements, which gives a
compact representation.

\begin{convention}[Downsets and antichains]
\label{conv:downsets}
For a poset $(O,\preceq)$ and $o\in O$, write
$\mathord{\downarrow}o = \{o'\in O : o'\preceq o\}$, and for
$A\subseteq O$ write
$\mathord{\downarrow}A=\bigcup_{o\in A}\mathord{\downarrow}o$.
A set $D\subseteq O$ is a \emph{downset} if
$D = \mathord{\downarrow}D$; we write $\Down(O)$ for the set of
downsets of $O$.
An \emph{antichain} is a set of pairwise incomparable elements; we
write $\Ant(O)$ for the antichains of $O$.
Since $O$ is finite, the maps
$D\mapsto\max(D)$ and $A\mapsto\mathord{\downarrow}A$
are mutually inverse bijections between $\Down(O)$ and $\Ant(O)$.
\end{convention}

Figure~\ref{fig:antichains} illustrates the correspondence: each
antichain generates its downset, and each downset is recovered from
its maximal elements.

\begin{definition}[Ordered bundle domain]
\label{def:down-bundles}
Over an ordered property domain $(O,\preceq)$, a remaining-interest
assignment takes values in $\Down(O)$, and the bundle domain at
subgoal $x$ is
\[
Q_x = \Down(I(x)),
\]
the downsets of the restriction of $(O,\preceq)$ to $I(x)$, ordered
by inclusion $\sqsubseteq$, with bottom $\varnothing$ and join
$D_1\sqcup D_2 = D_1\cup D_2$.
In antichain representation (Convention~\ref{conv:downsets}), a
bundle is its antichain of maximal elements, and
\[
A_1\sqcup A_2 = \max(A_1\cup A_2),
\]
i.e., union followed by removal of subsumed elements.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Conservativity]
\label{rem:conservative}
When $O$ is discrete (trivially ordered), every subset is a downset,
so $\Down(O)=\mathcal P(O)$ and the definitions of this subsection
collapse to the Boolean model of
Sections~\ref{sec:properties}--\ref{sec:bundles}.
Conversely, nothing downstream needs revisiting: the definitions of
labels, dominance, semantic factorization, and the lifted recurrence
(Sections~\ref{sec:costs}--\ref{sec:recurrence}), together with
Lemma~\ref{lem:restriction}, use only the join-semilattice structure
$(\sqsubseteq,\sqcup,\varnothing)$ of $Q_x$ and the restriction maps
$D\mapsto D\cap I(y)$, all of which are available verbatim over
downsets.
Theorem~\ref{thm:free} in the next section explains in what sense
this completion is canonical.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}
In the domain of Example~\ref{ex:refinable-properties}, the bundle
$\mathord{\downarrow}\{\mathrm{sorted}(a,b)\}$ contains
$\mathrm{sorted}(a)$ automatically: a plan advertising the refined
order satisfies every requirement on the coarser one, with no
bookkeeping in $\lambda$ or $F_r$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Continuation Equivalence}
\label{sec:continuation}

The definitions so far take the property domain and labeling as
given.
To \emph{characterize} Bellman's principle---rather than merely
exploit it---we need an intrinsic notion of when two solutions are
interchangeable, defined without reference to any chosen property
vocabulary.
The role of ``the future of a solution'' is played by
\emph{contexts}: solutions of the goal with a hole into which a
solution of $x$ may be plugged.

\begin{definition}[Contexts]
\label{def:contexts}
For a subgoal $x$, a \emph{context} for $x$ is a solution term for
the goal $\goal$ with a single hole of sort $x$: formally, an element
of $\Sol(\goal)$ in the decomposition system extended with a fresh
primitive solution $[\,\cdot\,]_x\in\Prim(x)$ that is used exactly
once.
Write $\Ctx(x)$ for the set of contexts for $x$, and $C[s]$ for the
solution obtained by plugging $s\in\Sol(x)$ into the hole.
\end{definition}

We then deem two solutions equivalent when swapping one for the
other inside any complete solution leaves the rest of the cost
unchanged: no context can tell them apart, so no optimizer ever
needs to distinguish them.
Collecting the solutions of $x$ into equivalence classes yields a
\emph{quotient} of $\Sol(x)$: the set whose elements are the
classes themselves.
Readers who recall automata theory will recognize a
Myhill--Nerode-style construction: it quotients solutions by their
future behavior, just as Myhill--Nerode quotients string prefixes by
their acceptance futures.

\begin{definition}[Continuation equivalence]
\label{def:continuation}
For $s,t\in\Sol(x)$, define
\[
s \equiv_x t
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\forall C\in\Ctx(x):\;
c_{\goal}(C[s]) - c_x(s)
\;=\;
c_{\goal}(C[t]) - c_x(t),
\]
with the convention that $\infty-\infty$ cases are handled by
requiring instead that feasibility and incremental cost agree.
The quotient
\[
K_x = \Sol(x)/{\equiv_x}
\]
is the \emph{continuation quotient} at $x$.
\end{definition}

Continuation equivalence gives a yardstick for labelings.
A labeling is \emph{sufficient} when it refines the continuation
quotient---it may distinguish more than necessary, but never
less---and \emph{exact} when it distinguishes precisely what the
quotient does.

\begin{definition}[Sufficient and exact labelings]
\label{def:sufficient}
A label map $\lambda_x$ is \emph{Bellman-sufficient} when
\[
\lambda_x(s)=\lambda_x(t)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
s\equiv_x t,
\]
and \emph{exact} when the converse implication also holds, in which
case the image of $\lambda_x$ is in bijection with $K_x$ (restricted
to realized labels).
\end{definition}

Selinger's interesting orders are a Bellman-sufficient labeling for
System~R's cost model; whether they are exact depends on the operator
inventory.
The conjectures of Section~\ref{sec:results} assert that $K_x$ is
itself the canonical semantic domain.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We organize the intended results as a ladder: an exactness theorem
whose proof is routine but which isolates the Bellman condition; a
free-completion theorem explaining why bundle domains and antichain
frontiers are canonical; the recovery of Selinger's algorithm as the
discrete instance; two conjectures---universality and
minimality---that would characterize Bellman's principle in full;
and a quantitative agenda: since every decomposition system can be
lifted to satisfy Bellman's principle
(Proposition~\ref{prop:trivial}), the
intrinsic measures of a problem are the \emph{width} and
\emph{dimension} of a minimal sufficient frontier.

\subsection{Exactness of Semantic Memoization}

\begin{theorem}[Bellman sufficiency]
\label{thm:exactness}
Let $(X,\mathcal R,\goal)$ be a decomposition system with a semantic
labeling that factors it (Definition~\ref{def:factorization}).
Then for every subgoal $x$ and every bundle $q\in Q_x$,
\[
\widehat V_x(q) = V_x(q).
\]
In particular, $\widehat V_{\goal}(\varnothing)$ is the optimum cost
of the problem.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Structural induction over the decomposition DAG.

($\widehat V_x(q)\ge V_x(q)$, soundness.)
Every term considered by the recurrence corresponds to a concrete
solution: primitive solutions directly, and constructor terms by
choosing, for each child bundle $q_i$, a witness
$s_i$ with $q_i\sqsubseteq\lambda_{x_i}(s_i)$ and
$c_{x_i}(s_i)=\widehat V_{x_i}(q_i)$ (inductively $=V_{x_i}(q_i)$).
Monotonicity of $F_r$ gives
$q\sqsubseteq F_r(q_1,\ldots,q_k)
\sqsubseteq
F_r(\lambda(s_1),\ldots,\lambda(s_k))
=\lambda_x(r(s_1,\ldots,s_k))$,
so the constructed solution satisfies $q$.
Its cost is at most the value computed: the witnesses' labels
$\lambda_{x_i}(s_i)\sqsupseteq q_i$ cannot raise the cost
(antitonicity of $G_r$ in labels), and their costs equal the
memoized values (monotonicity of $G_r$ in costs).

($\widehat V_x(q)\le V_x(q)$, completeness.)
Let $s$ be a cheapest solution of $x$ satisfying $q$.
If $s$ is primitive, the first branch of the recurrence covers it.
Otherwise $s=r(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$; take
$q_i=\lambda_{x_i}(s_i)$.
Then $q\sqsubseteq\lambda_x(s)=F_r(q_1,\ldots,q_k)$, so this tuple is
enumerated.
By induction $\widehat V_{x_i}(q_i)\le c_{x_i}(s_i)$, and
monotonicity of $G_r$ yields a recurrence value at most $c_x(s)$.
\end{proof}

The theorem is deliberately modest: it converts the informal
Principle of Optimality into a checkable condition on labelings
(factorization) and locates the entire burden there.
All domain-specific content of a Selinger-style optimizer resides in
the choice of $(O,\preceq)$, $I$, $F_r$, and $G_r$.

\subsection{The Bundle Domain is a Free Completion}

\begin{theorem}[Free join-semilattice completion]
\label{thm:free}
Let $(P,\preceq)$ be a finite poset.
Then $(\Down(P),\subseteq,\cup,\varnothing)$, together with the
embedding $\eta:P\to\Down(P)$, $\eta(p)=\mathord{\downarrow}p$, is
the free join-semilattice (with bottom) on $P$: for every
join-semilattice $L$ with bottom and every monotone map
$f:P\to L$, there is a unique bottom- and join-preserving
homomorphism $\widehat f:\Down(P)\to L$ with
$\widehat f\circ\eta=f$, namely
\[
\widehat f(D)=\bigvee_{p\in\max(D)}f(p).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Standard.
Well-definedness and homomorphism properties follow from
$D=\bigcup_{p\in\max D}\mathord{\downarrow}p$ and monotonicity of
$f$; uniqueness because the $\eta(p)$ generate $\Down(P)$ under
finite joins.
\end{proof}

In plain terms: the powerset and downset constructions add exactly
one capability to the property domain---the ability to hold several
incomparable alternatives open at once---and nothing else.
Any summary domain that can combine alternatives at all must contain
this one; any smaller summary must conflate alternatives that some
constructor may later treat differently
(Remark~\ref{rem:top} below makes the failure concrete).
This is the precise sense in which the memo tables of
Section~\ref{sec:foundations} are forced rather than chosen.

\begin{corollary}[Discrete case]
\label{cor:discrete}
When $P$ is discrete, $\Down(P)=\mathcal P(P)$: the free
join-semilattice on a set is its powerset under union.
Hence the Boolean bundle domains of Definition~\ref{def:bundles} and
the ordered bundle domains of Definition~\ref{def:down-bundles} are
the same construction at different degrees of semantic refinement,
and the passage from the Boolean model to the ordered model in
Section~\ref{sec:refinement} is not an ad hoc generalization but the
free completion of the refinement order.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Why a top element fails]
\label{rem:top}
Freeness explains a design point.
One might hope to summarize incomparable alternatives
$\{p_1,\ldots,p_k\}$ by a single formal top element
(``undetermined'').
But collapsing all antichains to $\top$ identifies
$\{a,b\}$, $\{a,c\}$, and $\{a,b,c\}$, so property-transfer maps can
no longer act differently on them, and factorization
(Definition~\ref{def:factorization}) fails for any constructor that
consumes one alternative but not another.
The downset completion adds exactly the joins forced by incomparable
bundles and nothing else; it is the least completion compatible with
deferred commitment.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Selinger as the Discrete Instance}

\begin{proposition}[System~R]
\label{prop:selinger}
Selinger-style join-order optimization with interesting orders is the
instance of Theorem~\ref{thm:exactness} with:
$X$ the connected subexpressions of the join graph;
constructors the physical join operators;
$O$ the discrete poset of canonicalized sort keys mentioned in join
predicates and \textup{ORDER BY}/\textup{GROUP BY} clauses;
$I(x)$ the keys occurring in predicates and clauses not yet applied
at $x$;
$\lambda$ the advertised output orders of plans;
$F_r$ the standard physical-property propagation rules; and
$G_r$ the standard cost model.
The interesting-order memo table of System~R computes exactly the
frontier of Definition~\ref{def:dominance}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof obligations]
Routine but worth recording in full: (i) the property propagation of
each physical operator factors through output-order labels
(Definition~\ref{def:factorization}(1)); (ii) estimated operator
costs are monotone in child costs
(Definition~\ref{def:factorization}(2)); (iii) $I$ is antitone and
exhausted at the root.
Example~\ref{ex:transfer} verifies (i) for nested-loop and merge
joins in the running example.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Universality}

Theorem~\ref{thm:exactness} shows that a semantic factorization
suffices for Bellman's principle.
The deeper question is necessity: is \emph{every} dynamic program
that satisfies Bellman's principle of this form?
To pose the question precisely we use the intrinsic quotient of
Section~\ref{sec:continuation}.

\begin{conjecture}[Universality]
\label{conj:universality}
Let $(X,\mathcal R,\goal)$ be a decomposition system with costs
$c_x:\Sol(x)\to\Rbar$ (and no given labeling).
Then the continuation quotients $K_x$ support a semantic
factorization: there exist a finite ordered property domain
$(O,\preceq)$,
an antitone remaining-interest assignment $I$, and an exact labeling
$\lambda$ (Definition~\ref{def:sufficient}) factoring the system,
with $Q_x$-labels in bijection with the realized classes of $K_x$.
Consequently, a decomposition problem admits an exact
property-indexed dynamic program if and only if its continuation
quotients are (uniformly) small.
\end{conjecture}

The ``if'' direction is Theorem~\ref{thm:exactness}.
The substance is the converse: constructing $(O,\preceq)$ and $I$
from the quotients $K_x$ alone.
We expect the construction to hinge on one structural condition
beyond monotonicity: \emph{incremental} Bellman compatibility, the
requirement that retaining two alternative bundles and constructing
afterward agrees with constructing from each bundle and joining the
results.

\begin{definition}[Join-compositionality]
\label{def:join-comp}
A property-transfer map $F_r$ is \emph{join-compositional} when it
preserves joins separately in each argument:
\[
F_r(\ldots,q_i\sqcup q_i',\ldots)
=
F_r(\ldots,q_i,\ldots)\sqcup F_r(\ldots,q_i',\ldots).
\]
\end{definition}

The natural candidate is to let properties be (generated by) the
continuation classes themselves and to let $I$ record which classes
remain distinguishable above each subgoal; the work is to show that
constructors descend to the quotients and that the resulting
transfer maps are monotone and, we expect, join-compositional.

\subsection{Minimality}

Among Bellman-sufficient labelings, the continuation quotient should
be canonical.

\begin{conjecture}[Minimality]
\label{conj:minimality}
The quotient map $\kappa_x:\Sol(x)\to K_x$ is the coarsest
Bellman-sufficient summary: for every Bellman-sufficient labeling
$\lambda_x:\Sol(x)\to Q_x$ there is a unique map
$h_x:\lambda_x(\Sol(x))\to K_x$ with
$\kappa_x = h_x\circ\lambda_x$, and the family $\{h_x\}$ commutes
with the property-transfer maps and restriction maps.
Thus every semantic factorization factors through the continuation
quotients, which are initial among Bellman-valid semantic domains.
\end{conjecture}

Conjectures~\ref{conj:universality} and~\ref{conj:minimality}
together would give a Myhill--Nerode theorem for dynamic programming:
Bellman's principle holds exactly when future distinguishability has
a finite compositional representation, and the continuation quotient
is its minimal automaton.
Selinger's interesting orders, Cascades' property requests, and
e-graph extraction frontiers would then be different coordinate
systems on the same canonical object.

\subsection{The Price of Bellman: Width and Dimension}
\label{sec:width}

The informal claim of Section~\ref{sec:motivation} was deliberately
strong: every compositionally presented problem can be made to
satisfy Bellman's principle.
That is not a conjecture; it is a nearly trivial proposition, and
its triviality is the point.
One honest hedge is required: the framework takes the
decomposition---the subgoals and constructors---as given, and
choosing a good decomposition is itself part of the art, outside the
scope of the present theory.
Within any fixed decomposition, however, sufficiency is automatic.

Here is the simple idea underneath the syntax.
Bellman's principle fails when a summary forgets something the
future can see.
A summary that forgets \emph{nothing} therefore cannot fail: let
each solution's ``property'' be its own identity, so that a
solution's label says, in effect, ``I am exactly this solution.''
Labels then trivially determine the label and cost of anything built
from them, which is all that factorization demands.
The formal statement below merely arranges these identity properties
to meet the bookkeeping requirements of
Definition~\ref{def:interest}: interest must shrink going up (so a
solution's identity is ``relevant'' at all subgoals below its own)
and must be empty at the goal (so goal solutions are labeled by
nothing, which is harmless because nothing is built from them).

\begin{proposition}[Sufficiency is always achievable]
\label{prop:trivial}
Every decomposition system with costs admits a semantic
factorization.
In particular, take
$O = \{(x,s) : x\in X\setminus\{\goal\},\ s\in\Sol(x)\}$,
$I(x) = \{(y,s)\in O : x\le_X y\}$, and
$\lambda_x(s)=\{(x,s)\}$ for $x\neq\goal$
(with $\lambda_{\goal}\equiv\varnothing$).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Antitonicity of $I$ is transitivity of $\le_X$, and $I(\goal)$
avoids the excluded goal pairs.
Each label identifies its solution exactly, so
$F_r(\{(x_1,s_1)\},\ldots,\{(x_k,s_k)\})
=\{(x,\,r(s_1,\ldots,s_k))\}$ (or $\varnothing$ at the goal) and
$G_r$ reconstructs the composite term and evaluates its cost;
both factor through labels by construction.
\end{proof}

This lift is useless in practice: the memo tables are as large as
the solution spaces themselves.
The lesson is that Bellman's principle is never \emph{unavailable};
it is only \emph{expensive}.
The substantive question is quantitative---how large must a
sufficient frontier be?---and the continuation quotient supplies the
yardstick.

\begin{definition}[Bellman width]
\label{def:width}
The \emph{width} of a subgoal $x$ is $w(x)=|K_x|$, the number of
continuation classes at $x$; the width of the problem is
$\max_{x\in X} w(x)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Bellman dimension]
\label{def:dimension}
The \emph{(Boolean) dimension} of a problem is the least $|O|$ over
all Boolean semantic factorizations whose labeling is
Bellman-sufficient; the \emph{ordered dimension} is defined likewise
over ordered property domains.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Basic bounds; to be proved in full]
\label{prop:bounds}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Every Bellman-sufficient labeling realizes at least $w(x)$
  distinct labels at $x$; hence $w(x)$ lower-bounds the size of any
  exact memo table at $x$.
\item In the Boolean model, realized labels at $x$ are subsets of
  $I(x)$, so $|I(x)|\ge\log_2 w(x)$, and the Boolean dimension is at
  least $\max_x\lceil\log_2 w(x)\rceil$.
\item The lifted recurrence runs in time
  $O\bigl(\sum_{r:(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x}\prod_{i} f(x_i)\bigr)$
  evaluations of the transfer and cost maps, where $f(x_i)$ is the
  frontier size at $x_i$.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, dynamic programming is efficient exactly when widths
are small: polynomial width gives polynomial-size memo tables, and
exponential width forbids them.
\end{proposition}

\begin{example}[Held--Karp width]
\label{ex:heldkarp}
The traveling salesperson problem fits the framework with subgoals
$x_k$ = ``simple paths visiting $k$ cities from a fixed start,''
constructors extending a path by one city, and the goal being
complete tours.
For generic edge weights, two partial paths are
continuation-equivalent if and only if they visit the same city set
and end at the same city, so
$w(x_k)=\binom{n}{k}\,k$: the width is exponential in $n$.
The Held--Karp dynamic program~\cite{heldkarp1962} memoizes exactly
these classes, so it is the canonical lift---and its $2^n$ table is
not an artifact of a weak state choice but the price the problem
demands (at least among property-indexed dynamic programs).
Selinger's problem, by contrast, has width bounded by the number of
realizable order bundles, which is small for typical queries.
Characterizing which problems have polynomial width is, we believe,
the right abstract form of the question ``when does dynamic
programming work?''
\end{example}

\begin{openproblem}[Compositionality gap]
\label{open:gap}
The bound of Proposition~\ref{prop:bounds}(2) is
information-theoretic: $\lceil\log_2 w\rceil$ properties suffice to
\emph{name} the continuation classes, but labels must also factor
through every constructor
(Definition~\ref{def:factorization}), which may force more
properties.
Determine how large the gap between the compositional dimension and
$\lceil\log_2 w\rceil$ can be, and identify structural conditions
under which the information-theoretic bound is achieved.
\end{openproblem}

\begin{openproblem}[Small-width problems]
\label{open:width}
Characterize the decomposition systems (or problem families) of
polynomial width.
Such a characterization would delimit, in one vocabulary, the
problems for which Selinger-style dynamic programming, Cascades-style
memoization, and frontier-indexed e-graph extraction can be
efficient.
\end{openproblem}

\subsection{Cost Terrains and Local Search}
\label{sec:terrain}

A third setting motivated this work: discrete non-convex
optimization over a \emph{cost terrain}, as navigated by local
search and Monte Carlo methods.
A terrain is a finite neighborhood graph with a cost on vertices.
Orienting each edge downhill (after contracting equal-cost plateaus)
yields an acyclic \emph{descent graph}; its sinks are the local
minima, and non-convexity is branching: a vertex whose descent paths
reach two different sinks.
Monte Carlo methods deliberately traverse a cyclic proposal graph,
but the cycles belong to the algorithm, not the problem; the
semantics below lives on the acyclic descent orientation.

The terrain maps into the framework directly.
Subgoals are the vertices reachable from a start vertex $v_0$; each
descent edge $x\to y$ contributes a unary constructor $(y)\to x$;
each sink $m$ has one primitive solution (``stop here,'' with cost
$c(m)$); the goal is $v_0$.
A solution of $x$ is a descending walk from $x$ to a sink, and its
cost is the cost of the sink it reaches.

\begin{remark}[Non-convexity is not width]
\label{rem:terrain-width}
For the standalone question---what is the best sink value reachable
from $v_0$?---every context merely prepends a descent prefix and
contributes nothing to cost, so \emph{all} solutions of a subgoal
are continuation-equivalent: $w(x)=1$, and the scalar recurrence
$V_x=\min_{x\to y}V_y$ is exact with no properties at all.
The multi-basin structure that defeats greedy descent is invisible
to offline memoization.
Non-convexity obstructs \emph{irrevocable} search; it is not, by
itself, a failure of Bellman's principle.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Embedding creates width]
\label{rem:terrain-embed}
Sink identity becomes continuation-relevant the moment the terrain
is embedded in a larger process: the walk continues from wherever it
lands (a restart, a warm start, a later optimization phase, a
composite objective).
Take the property domain $O$ to be the sinks, with sink identity
demanded by the enclosing goal.
The canonical label of a vertex is then its \emph{basin frontier}:
the Pareto-optimal pairs (sink, best cost reachable in that basin).
The transfer map of a descent edge is union followed by dominance
pruning---join-compositional---and the lifted recurrence specializes
to a single reverse-topological pass,
\[
F(x)=\bigsqcup_{x\to y}F(y),
\]
computable in linear time.
Width is the number of continuation-distinct basins: the branching
antichains of the terrain.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Three coordinate systems]
\label{rem:terrain-coords}
The same lift has three presentations.
As a \emph{state} lift, memoize pairs (vertex, basin).
As a \emph{cost} lift, replace the scalar objective by a vector with
one coordinate per live basin, with componentwise minimum as join;
smoothing a rugged optimization surface by adding cost dimensions,
in the spirit of Ioannidis and Kang's analysis of query optimization
terrains~\cite{ioannidis1991left}, is this construction.
As a \emph{semantic} lift, label vertices by frontier bundles as in
this paper.
We expect these to be formally interchangeable, and the standalone
version of the story---keeping several basins alive under
irrevocable moves---is the determination-theoretic face of the same
frontier (Section~\ref{sec:determination-bridge}): deferred
commitment, not memoization.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Mult-Egraph}
\label{sec:multegraph}

The framework prescribes a data structure for equality saturation.
An e-graph groups terms into classes by equivalence; a
\emph{mult-egraph} further partitions each class by semantic label,
so that a term's subclass records exactly the properties it
provides.
The exact-label partition is the general structure; the memo
frontier and the requirement-indexed table of
Section~\ref{sec:foundations} are derived views, obtained by
pruning and coarsening.

\begin{definition}[Mult-egraph]
\label{def:multegraph}
Let a decomposition system be presented as an e-graph
(Example~\ref{ex:egraph}) with a semantic labeling $\lambda$.
The \emph{mult-egraph} refines each e-class $x$ into
\emph{subclasses}
\[
x[q] \;=\; \{\,s\in\Sol(x) : \lambda_x(s)=q\,\},
\]
one for each realized label $q\in\lambda_x(\Sol(x))$.
An e-node $r$ whose children are drawn from subclasses
$x_1[q_1],\ldots,x_k[q_k]$ is placed in subclass
$x[F_r(q_1,\ldots,q_k)]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Well-definedness; to be proved]
\label{prop:multegraph}
The subclass placement of e-nodes is well defined---every choice of
terms from the child subclasses yields parent terms with a common
label---if and only if the labeling factors
(Definition~\ref{def:factorization}(1)).
The mult-egraph is thus the data-structure form of the Bellman
condition: it exists as a coherent refinement of the e-graph exactly
when semantic memoization is exact.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Minimization]
\label{rem:multegraph-min}
Two coarsenings of the exact-label structure are useful in practice.
First, \emph{Pareto pruning}: extraction needs only a cheapest
representative per subclass, and subclasses dominated in both cost
and label (Remark~\ref{rem:pareto}) can be dropped; the survivors
are the memo frontier, and a bundle requirement $q$ is answered by
the cheapest surviving subclass whose label covers $q$.
Second, the \emph{requirement quotient}: subclasses that no
continuation can distinguish may be merged, and the coarsest correct
merge is the continuation quotient $K_x$
(Conjecture~\ref{conj:minimality}).
A mult-egraph finer than $K_x$ wastes space; one coarser extracts
suboptimally.
Finally, the nested, coarser views of a mult-egraph arise by
forgetting properties: the restriction maps of
Definition~\ref{def:restriction} coarsen the partition, and the
tower of views along the subgoal order is exactly the monotone
shrinkage of Section~\ref{sec:interest}.
\end{remark}

\begin{openproblem}
\label{open:egraph}
Construct an explicit equality-saturation example in which e-classes
are strictly coarser than continuation equivalence---i.e., ordinary
single-representative extraction is inexact---and exhibit a
property domain $O$ of extraction-relevant properties
(streamability, partition-preservation, sortedness, \ldots) whose
semantic factorization restores exactness via
Theorem~\ref{thm:exactness}.
Beyond validating the framework, this is the design brief for a
follow-on to \textsf{egg} built on the mult-egraph: an extraction
engine that takes $(O,\preceq,I,F_r,G_r)$ as input and thereby
subsumes Cascades-style optimizers as one configuration.
\end{openproblem}

\subsection{Demand Transformation}
\label{sec:demand}

The requirement bundles of Section~\ref{sec:bundles} are demands,
and there is a classical theory of demand in bottom-up evaluation:
magic-sets rewriting~\cite{bancilhon1986magic} and its refinement,
demand transformation~\cite{tekle2011subsumptive}.
There, an \emph{adornment} describes what a calling context demands
of a subgoal, and the rewrite specializes each predicate by
adornment so that bottom-up evaluation computes only demanded facts.
The correspondence with the present framework is close: adorned
predicates are pairs (predicate, demand descriptor) exactly as our
lifted memo states are pairs $(x,q)$.
One notable difference: magic sets specializes on \emph{values}
(which tuples) to prune irrelevant computation, while we specialize
on \emph{properties} (how outputs are shaped) to restore Bellman's
principle---the same rewrite, deployed for relevance in one case and
sufficiency in the other.
We see three uses for the correspondence.

First, it derives the remaining-interest assignment.
Definition~\ref{def:interest} takes $I$ as given; the demand
transformation computes it.
Define the \emph{demand closure} $D$ as the least set of pairs such
that $(\goal,\varnothing)\in D$, and whenever $(x,q)\in D$ and
$r:(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\to x$ admits child bundles with
$q\sqsubseteq F_r(q_1,\ldots,q_k)$, each $(x_i,q_i)\in D$.
Restricting the lifted recurrence to $D$ should preserve
$\widehat V_{\goal}(\varnothing)$, and
$I(x)=\bigcup\{q : (x,q)\in D\}$ is a candidate constructive answer
to where $I$ comes from---relevant to the construction demanded by
Conjecture~\ref{conj:universality}.

Second, it names a duality we have circled repeatedly:
\emph{Cascades is the magic-sets rewriting of Selinger}.
Top-down optimizers propagate required physical properties from
parent to child with memoization; that is demand-transformed
evaluation of the lifted bottom-up recurrence.
The bottom-up and top-down architectures are not merely analogous;
they should be related by a rewrite with a classical correctness
proof pattern.

Third, it is load-bearing for the mult-egraph
(Section~\ref{sec:multegraph}): the exact-label partition can be
large, and the demand closure prescribes which subclasses to
materialize.
Goal-directed extraction is demand-transformed extraction.
Suggestively, the result that \emph{subsumptive} tabling beats magic
sets~\cite{tekle2011subsumptive} maps onto our satisfaction
relation: answering a demand $q$ from a stronger cached supply
$\lambda(s)\sqsupseteq q$ is subsumption, so dominance-aware
memoization is the optimization their analysis rewards.

\begin{remark}[Demand can overshoot supply]
\label{rem:demand-supply}
The demand closure as defined ranges over \emph{all} child bundles
admitted by the transfer maps, and may therefore demand bundles that
no solution supplies: in the running example, a top merge join on
$b$ demands $(RS,\{b\})$, which Example~\ref{ex:crank} showed to be
unrealizable.
The practically right object is the intersection of the demand
closure, computed top-down, with realized supply, computed
bottom-up---which is exactly what a Cascades-style memo achieves
operationally and what subsumptive tabling approximates.
Any minimality claim about the induced $I$ must be stated relative
to this intersection, not to demand alone.
\end{remark}

\begin{openproblem}[Demand transformation of the lifted recurrence]
\label{open:demand}
Formalize the demand closure, prove that restriction to it preserves
the goal value, show that the induced $I$ is the least antitone
remaining-interest assignment supporting the goal demand, and
determine whether the complexity guarantees of demand transformation
and subsumptive tabling transfer to property-indexed dynamic
programs.
\end{openproblem}

\subsection{Relation to Determination Theory}
\label{sec:determination-bridge}

We have kept the development self-contained, but the correspondence
flagged in Remark~\ref{rem:determination} deserves a precise
statement.
In determination theory, a specification presents a space of
admissible outcomes whose ambiguity is resolved by a sequence of
commitments; each commitment shrinks ambiguity monotonically, and
static determination rewrites the specification before execution so
that fewer commitments remain at runtime.
Here, $I(x)$ behaves as the remaining ambiguity over observations, the
antitone shrinkage of Definition~\ref{def:interest} behaves as a
filtration, and the lift from single solutions to frontier-indexed
memo states is a static determination: it replaces a premature
commitment (``keep one best plan'') with an explicit representation
of deferred alternatives.
We conjecture, informally, that semantic dynamic programming is the
special case of static determination in which the specification is
an optimization problem over a decomposition system, and that the
Persistence and monotonicity properties of determination filtrations
specialize to Lemma~\ref{lem:restriction}.
Making this precise---in particular, relating the continuation
quotient $K_x$ to support quotients---is future work.
