\section{Which Distinctions Must an Optimizer Preserve?}
\label{sec:intro-preserve}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

A broad family of discrete optimization problems can be characterized
as encompassing a hierarchy of subproblems to optimize.
A subproblem may admit many solutions.
Retaining all of them is correct but often impractical.
Retaining only one locally preferred solution is efficient but
generally unsound.
An optimizer for these settings needs a principled way to decide which
distinctions among solutions must be preserved.

Relational database query optimization provides a familiar example.
For a fixed relational subquery, many physical plans compute the
same logical result.
Some differ only in properties such as output order.
A subplan that is more expensive in isolation may nevertheless be
the best input to a larger optimal plan because an enclosing operator
can exploit that order.
Selinger's treatment of interesting orders preserves exactly
such distinctions: plans for the same subquery are kept
separate only when a larger expression may distinguish them.

The same issue appears in equality saturation.
An e-class records alternatives that are equivalent at one
semantic level, while analyses or nested equivalences may retain
finer distinctions needed by surrounding expressions.
Again, the difficulty is not merely to enumerate alternatives,
but to determine the coarsest equivalence under which future
composition remains correct.

These notes study that problem directly.

We begin from a static semantic object rather than from a search
algorithm.
The object has two orthogonal components.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

\item
A hierarchy of semantic equivalences groups alternatives at
multiple resolutions.
We call its classes \emph{multe-classes}.

\item
A compositional graph records how a non-atomic alternative
depends on multe-classes of subgoals.
As in an ordinary e-graph, a parent e-node references child
e-classes rather than child e-nodes.

\end{enumerate}

Their combination is a \emph{multe-graph}.
The multe component records which alternatives are equivalent at
each semantic resolution.
The graph component records where those distinctions may be
observed by composition.
Neither component, by itself, specifies an optimization
objective.

A multe-graph is therefore a schema for a family of related
problems.
A particular problem instance is obtained by imposing
requirements on that schema.
For example, the queries

\[
\texttt{SELECT * FROM R JOIN S}
\]

and

\[
\texttt{SELECT * FROM R JOIN S ORDER BY R.a}
\]

may share one underlying multe-graph.
The second query adds a requirement at the root.
Other requirements may be attached elsewhere.
A memory-constrained instance might, for example, forbid
implementations that sort their inputs.

Requirements restrict the admissible relation represented by
the schema.
Because parent e-nodes refer to child multe-classes, local
requirements propagate through the graph.
After propagation, some e-nodes have been removed and some
semantic boundaries have become unobservable under the requirements.
Those boundaries may be collapsed.
The remaining non-empty multe-classes form the semantic state that must be
retained for the instance.

Only after this instantiation step do we choose
representatives for the remaining e-classes.
We call that second operation \emph{canonicalization}.
The separation is important:

\begin{description}[style=nextline]

\item[Requirements]
remove alternatives from the \emph{admissible} relation mapping an e-class
to its e-nodes.

\item[Canonicalization]
reduces each remaining relation to the unique representation 
desired for the application.

\end{description}

Scalar optimization is one canonicalization discipline.
A cost order may entail a unique optimum in each surviving
multe-class.
Pareto optimization is another.
It lifts the outcome domain from individual alternatives to
sets of undominated alternatives; the Pareto frontier is then a
uniquely entailed outcome in the lifted domain. These are the
standard settings for optimization. 
Determination Theory applies when several admissible outcomes remain
and no semantic order entails a unique one; choosing among them
requires a \emph{commitment}; we leave this topic aside in the remainder.

The present document develops this decomposition in a
simplest-first order.
We begin with finite, acyclic schemas, tree-shaped
multe-class hierarchies, and Boolean requirements.
After the basic objects and candidate theorems are clear, we
relax these assumptions.

The main questions are then precise.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

\item
What local conditions make a multe-graph a sound semantic
schema?

\item
When does requirement propagation have a unique least fixed
point?

\item
Is the propagated structure the coarsest quotient that
preserves every distinction observable under the requirements?

\item
Under what conditions is one canonical representative per
surviving multe-class sufficient?

\end{enumerate}

A positive answer to the last question recovers Bellman's
Principle of Optimality as a consequence of the semantic
structure rather than as an assumption about a particular search
procedure.

\section{Goals and E-Nodes}
\label{sec:goals-enodes}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We begin with the local objects from which a semantic schema is
assembled.

At this stage, we deliberately ignore equivalence, composition,
requirements, costs, and canonicalization.  We assume only that a
problem can be divided into named goals, and that each goal admits a
collection of alternative realizations.

\begin{definition}[Goal]
A \emph{goal} is a semantic task whose admissible realizations are to
be represented by the schema.
\end{definition}

The word ``goal'' is intentionally neutral.  Depending on the
application, a goal may be

\begin{itemize}
\item a relational expression to be implemented;
\item a program expression to be rewritten;
\item a logical proposition to be established;
\item an output value to be produced; or
\item an abstract capability to be provided.
\end{itemize}

A goal specifies \emph{what} is to be realized, not how it is realized.

Let \(X\) be a finite set of goals.  For each \(x \in X\), let
\[
\mathsf{N}(x)
\]
denote the finite set of e-nodes associated with \(x\).

\begin{definition}[E-node]
An \emph{e-node} for a goal \(x\) is one admissible local realization
of \(x\).
\end{definition}

Thus every e-node belongs to exactly one goal.  We write
\[
n : x
\]
to mean that \(n \in \mathsf{N}(x)\).

An e-node is a local realization of its goal.  A non-atomic e-node
specifies its operator and the semantic requirements on its children,
without selecting particular child realizations.

For now, we distinguish only two cases.

\begin{definition}[Atomic and non-atomic e-nodes]
An e-node is \emph{atomic} if its realization requires no subordinate
goals.  It is \emph{non-atomic} otherwise.
\end{definition}

This distinction records whether an alternative is compositionally
closed, but does not yet specify its children.

\begin{example}[Relational query optimization]
Consider the goal
\[
x_{R \Join S}
\]
of producing the relation \(R \Join S\).

Possible e-nodes for this goal include
\[
\mathsf{HashJoin(x_R, x_S)},
\qquad
\mathsf{MergeJoin(x_R, x_S)},
\qquad
\mathsf{NestedLoopJoin(x_R, x_S)}.
\]

These are different local realizations of the same goal.  They are
non-atomic because each depends on realizations of the input goals
\(x_R\) and \(x_S\).

A table scan for \(R\), by contrast, may be represented by an atomic
e-node for the goal \(x_R\).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Equality saturation]
For the goal represented by the expression \(a(b+c)\), possible
e-nodes may include
\[
\mathsf{Mul}(a,b+c)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mathsf{Add}(ab,ac).
\]

Both realize the same goal.  Their subordinate structure will later be
represented through references to semantic classes of child goals.
\end{example}

At this point, the schema contains only the disjoint family
\[
\{\mathsf{N}(x)\}_{x\in X}
\]
of alternatives associated with each goal.

Nothing yet identifies two e-nodes as equivalent, and nothing yet says
how a non-atomic e-node depends on subordinate alternatives.  Those two
pieces of structure will be introduced separately:

\begin{enumerate}
\item multe-classes will identify semantically interchangeable
      alternatives; and
\item the multe-graph will allow non-atomic e-nodes to reference
      multe-classes of subordinate goals.
\end{enumerate}

Keeping these steps separate is important.  Goals and e-nodes describe
the local vocabulary of the schema.  Equivalence and composition are
additional structures imposed on that vocabulary.

\section{Composition}
\label{sec:composition}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Goals and e-nodes describe the local alternatives available to a
schema.  We next describe how alternatives for one goal may be composed
from alternatives for other goals.

We begin with a concrete, unquotiented account of composition.  This
will later provide the semantic relation represented more compactly by
references to multe-classes.

Let \(X\) be the set of goals, and let \(\mathsf{N}(x)\) be the set of
e-nodes for each goal \(x \in X\).

\begin{definition}[Composition signature]
A \emph{composition signature} is a tuple
\[
d=(x \Rightarrow x_1,\ldots,x_k)
\]
consisting of a parent goal \(x\) and an ordered list of subgoals
\(x_1,\ldots,x_k\).
\end{definition}

A composition signature specifies only the types of the participating
alternatives.  It does not yet say which combinations are semantically
admissible.

\begin{definition}[Composition relation]
For a composition signature
\[
d=(x \Rightarrow x_1,\ldots,x_k),
\]
a \emph{composition relation} is a relation
\[
\mathcal{R}_d
\subseteq
\mathsf{N}(x)
\times
\mathsf{N}(x_1)
\times\cdots\times
\mathsf{N}(x_k).
\]

A tuple
\[
(n,n_1,\ldots,n_k)\in\mathcal{R}_d
\]
states that the parent alternative \(n\) may be realized using the
subgoal alternatives \(n_1,\ldots,n_k\).
\end{definition}

Composition is relational rather than functional.

For a given parent goal \(x\), there may be many admissible
configurations of subgoals.  These may differ in both the parent
alternative and the alternatives chosen for its subgoals:
\[
(n,n_1,\ldots,n_k)\in\mathcal{R}_d,
\qquad
(n',n'_1,\ldots,n'_k)\in\mathcal{R}_{d'}.
\]

For example, the goal \(R\Join S\) may be realized by a hash join over
arbitrary implementations of \(R\) and \(S\), or by a merge join over
implementations that provide suitable orders.

Conversely, one parent alternative may admit several child
realizations:
\[
(n,n_1,\ldots,n_k) \in \mathcal{R}_d,
\qquad
(n,n'_1,\ldots,n'_k) \in \mathcal{R}_d.
\]
The parent e-node records a local semantic alternative, not necessarily
a unique derivation from concrete children.

This relational treatment separates three matters that are often
conflated:

\begin{enumerate}

\item
\emph{semantic admissibility}: whether a tuple belongs to
\(\mathcal{R}_d\);

\item
\emph{evaluation direction}: whether an implementation explores the
relation from parent to children, from children to parent, or in both
directions; and

\item
\emph{preference}: whether one admissible tuple is cheaper or otherwise
preferred to another.

\end{enumerate}

Only the first belongs to the semantic schema.  A composition relation
has no intrinsic traversal direction and contains no cost information.

\begin{example}[Join composition]
Let
\[
x_R,\qquad x_S,\qquad x_{R \Join S}
\]
be goals, and consider the composition form
\[
d_{\mathsf{join}}
=
( x_{R \Join S} \Rightarrow x_R,x_S ).
\]

The relation
\[
\mathcal{R}_{d_{\mathsf{join}}}
\subseteq
\mathsf{N}(x_{R \Join S})
\times
\mathsf{N}(x_R)
\times
\mathsf{N}(x_S)
\]
may contain tuples such as
\[
(\mathsf{HashJoin},r,s),
\]
\[
(\mathsf{MergeJoin},r',s'),
\]
and
\[
(\mathsf{NestedLoopJoin},r'',s'').
\]

Membership states only that the corresponding child alternatives may
be combined to realize the parent alternative.  It does not rank the
join methods or prescribe an order in which they are considered.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Expression composition]
For a binary addition form
\[
d_{+}
=
(x \Rightarrow x_1,x_2),
\]
the relation
\[
\mathcal{R}_{d_{+}}
\subseteq
\mathsf{N}(x)
\times
\mathsf{N}(x_1)
\times
\mathsf{N}(x_2)
\]
records which child alternatives may be combined by addition to realize
an alternative for \(x\).

Equivalent parent expressions may arise from different child tuples,
and the same child tuple may participate in several equivalent parent
representations.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Concrete semantic schema]
A \emph{concrete semantic schema} consists of

\begin{enumerate}

\item
a finite set of goals \(X\);

\item
a finite set of e-nodes \(\mathsf{N}(x)\) for each \(x \in X\);

\item
a finite collection of composition forms \(d\); and

\item
a composition relation \(\mathcal{R}_d\) for each form \(d\).

\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The concrete schema may be much larger than an optimizer should
materialize.  It distinguishes individual child e-nodes even when a
parent can observe only some coarser semantic property of them.

The next chapter introduces multe-classes.  Once those classes are
available, a non-atomic parent e-node will refer to child
multe-classes rather than to individual child e-nodes.  The resulting
multe-graph is a quotient representation of the concrete composition
relations defined here.
